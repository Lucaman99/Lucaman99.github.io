3 Ways to Think About QAOA
May 7th, 2020

Yes, now that you mention it, I do believe that I am somewhat obsessed with QAOA. What can I say, it's a cool algorithm! In my last blog post, I talked a lot about what QAOA can do, but I didn't give a lot motivation for why QAOA works (except for that tangent about non-commuting operators). I did write about this a decent bit back in late 2019, in my CV QAOA Notebook, however, I only discussed one way, out of the three ways I believe exist to conceptualize QAOA. The first is from the perspective ofthe Quantum Adiabatic Theorem, where QAOA is essentially an approximation of the adiabatic time-evolution from the grund state of an initial "simmple" Hamiltonian, to the ground state of a more complex one. The second way is by thinkig about QAOA almost as a "sifter" and a "shaker" (this analogy will make a lot more sense later in the post). The third way is thinking about QAOA as gradient descent in superposition. I frankly don't know if anyone has thought about QAOA in this way before (othr than in the paper on CV QAOA), but in my opinion, it is a nifty way to think about why the algorithm actually works!


Method 3: Gradient Descent in Superposition

We begin by considering the discrete unitary evolution of the $Z$ operator under the mixer unitary, which we choose to be the simple sum of $X$ gates acting on each qubit:

$$U^{\dagger} Z U \ = \ e^{i \alpha H_M} Z e^{-i \alpha H_M} \ = \ e^{i \alpha X} Z e^{-i \alpha X}$$
Since $X^2 \ = \ \mathbb{I}$, we get:

$$e^{i \alpha X} \ = \ \cos \alpha \mathbb{I} \ + \ i \sin \alpha X$$
Thus, we have:

$$U^{\dagger} Z U \ = \ \big( \cos \alpha \mathbb{I} \ + \ i \sin \alpha X \big) Z \big( \cos \alpha \mathbb{I} \ - \ i \sin \alpha X \big) \ = \ \cos^2 \alpha Z \ + \ \sin^2 \alpha XZX \ + \ i \sin \alpha \cos \alpha (XZ \ - \ ZX)$$ $$\Rightarrow \ (\cos^2 \alpha \ - \ \sin^2 \alpha) Z \ + \ 2 \sin \alpha \cos \alpha Y \ = \ \cos 2\alpha Z \ + \ \sin 2 \alpha Y$$
Now, we can consider the action of the cost layer upon our resulting unitary. Since our cost Hamiltonian will be diagonal in the $Z$ basis, we have, for $H_C \ = \ \text{diag}(a, \ b)$:

$$e^{i \gamma H_C} \ = \ \text{diag} (e^{i \gamma a}, \ e^{i \gamma b})$$
